Monte Carlo Simulation

Monte Carlo simulation is a powerful tool to obtain numerical results from repeated random sampling. It enables us to compute integrals by drawing samples from an arbitrary density. Rejection Sampling (RS) and Importance Sampling (IS) are examples of Monte Carlo simulation techniques that are widely used.

In this lab, we will use simulation examples to review and compare the basic ideas of RS and IS, understand what situations are suitable for such techniques and what are the necessary conditions for them to be valid.

Rejection Sampling

What is RS?

x <- runif(1000,-1,1)
y <- runif(1000,-1,1)
idx <- (x^2 + y^2 < 1)

plot(x[idx], y[idx], xlab="x", ylab="y")
curve((  1 * (1 - x^2)^0.5 ), add=TRUE, from=-1 , to =1)
curve(( -1 * (1 - x^2)^0.5 ), add=TRUE, from=-1 , to =1)

When should we use RS?

  • want to sample from a distribution \(f(\theta)\) but we only know its kernal, e.g., for posterior distributions;
  • want to sample from a truncated pdf but do not want to use inverse cdf methods, e.g., to avoid intractable integrals;
  • want to sample from a distribution \(f\) which is a hard problem, but we know how to sample from another distribution \(g\) (a.k.a. envelop function/ candidate density) which is easy (if certain conditions are met for \(g\) and \(f\));
  • dimension of the problem is reasonably small, usually < 10.

What are the necessary conditions to use RS?

  • able to evaluate the target density \(f\), or at least its kernel;
  • the candidate density \(g\) has heavier tail compared to the target density \(f\) at least over the domain we are interested in; or, you should be able to find the finite ceiling constant \[ \begin{aligned} M = \sup_{x \in \mathcal{X}_f} \frac{f(x)}{g(x)} < \infty; \end{aligned} \]
  • \(g\) dominates \(f\), i.e., support of \(f \subset\) support of \(g\).

Algorithm

First find a valid ceiling constant \(M\), then

  • sample \(x \sim g\)
  • simulate \(u \sim unif(0,1)\)
  • accept if \[ \begin{aligned} u \le \frac{f(x)}{Mg(x)} \end{aligned} \]
  • iterate until enough samples

Comments on RS

  • main drawback of the approach: curse of dimensionality. Notice that as dimension grows, efficiency drops quickly;
  • once you find \(M\), all the values \(>=M\) can be used for RS, but using the smallest possible value would give us the highest acceptance rate;
  • always consider implementing your sampler on log scale for numerical stability.

Importance Sampling

RS vs IS

With RS, we get a sample from the target density \(f\). Samples give us great flexibility – we can basically evaluate any statistics regarding \(f\) from the sample. But sometimes we are only interested in computing certain expectations \(\mathsf{E}_f[h(X)]\), where \(f\) is the target density and \(h\) is some function \(h: \mathbb{R}^k \to \mathbb{R}\) (e.g., \(h(X)=X\) gives us the mean of \(f\)). While RS is a perfectly valid solution, for this much narrower problem, we can make use of Importance Sampling, which is more efficient.

What is IS?

The idea is, when we draw samples from the candidate density \(g\), most of the time, these are also possible values of the target density \(f\), but just appearing in the wrong frequency. So instead of throwing away some of these samples, we can downweight/ upweight them in order to get to the correct frequency under \(f\).

\[ \begin{aligned} \int f(x)h(x)dx &= \int \frac{f(x)}{g(x)} g(x) h(x)dx \\ \mathsf{E}_f[h(X)] &= \mathsf{E}_g \left[\frac{f(x)}{g(x)}h(x) \right]. \end{aligned} \]

Therefore if we are able to draw samples \(x_1, \dots, x_n\) from \(g\), \[ \begin{aligned} \frac 1n \sum_{i=1}^n \frac{f(x_i)}{g(x_i)} h(x_i) = \frac 1n \sum_{i=1}^n w_ih(x_i) \approx \mathsf{E}_f[h(X)]. \end{aligned} \]

Example: Bayesian Sensitivity Analysis

An interesting application of IS in Bayesian statistics is sensitivity analysis of posterior inferences with respect to prior parameter specification.

Suppose we observe data vector \(\mathbf{y} \sim f(\mathbf{y} \mid \theta)\). And we assume the parameter \(\theta\) follows prior distribution \(\pi(\theta \mid \psi_0)\) (note: both \(\theta\) and \(\psi_0\) here can be vectors). So the posterior distribution of \(\theta\) is

\[p(\theta \mid \mathbf{y}, \psi_0) \propto f(\mathbf{y} \mid \theta) \pi(\theta \mid \psi_0).\] We can draw samples from this posterior distribution and compute quantities of interests. For example, if we have drawn \(\theta_1, \dots, \theta_n\) from the posterior distribution, we can estimate the posterior mean as

\[\mathsf{E}[p(\theta \mid \mathbf{y}, \psi_0)] \approx \frac 1n \sum_{i=1}^n \theta_i.\]

Now suppose we are interested to know what the posterior mean would be if instead of using \(\psi_0\), we use \(\psi_1 \ne \psi_0\) as prior parameter. How would you calculate it? Of course we can repeat what we did in the first place. First rederive the posterior distribution

\[p(\theta \mid \mathbf{y}, \psi_1) \propto f(\mathbf{y} \mid \theta) \pi(\theta \mid \psi_1),\] then draw samples from it and use the sample mean as an estimate.

But leveraging on the concept of IS can save us the efforts of re-sampling. Consider \(p(\theta \mid \mathbf{y}, \psi_1)\) as our target density, and \(p(\theta \mid \mathbf{y}, \psi_0)\) the candidate density.

  1. If we know the full form of \(p(\theta \mid \mathbf{y}, \psi_0)\) and \(p(\theta \mid \mathbf{y}, \psi_1)\) (i.e., we know the normalizing constant), write down the formula of the IS estimator.

  2. Most of the time, we only know the posterior distributions up to a constant. Let \(p^*(\theta \mid \mathbf{y}, \psi_0)\) and \(p^*(\theta \mid \mathbf{y}, \psi_1)\) denote the kernels of the two posterior distributions, write down the formula of the IS estimator.

Comments on IS

  • also not suitable for high dimensional problems;
  • subject to the same conditions as RS;
  • the closer the candidate density is to the target density, the better the quality of the estimate.
